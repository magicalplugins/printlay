"What format should I send for DTF?" is one of the most common questions print shops get. The short answer: **a high-resolution file with transparency, at 300 DPI**. Here's how the main formats compare and when to use each.

## The two things that actually matter

Before format, get these right — they matter more than the file type:

- **Transparency** — the background must be transparent so only your design prints and white is laid only where needed. A solid background prints as a block.
- **Resolution** — **300 DPI at final print size**. Upscaling a small image doesn't add detail; start high-res. Unsure? Use the [bleed & DPI calculator](/tools/bleed-dpi-calculator).

## PNG — the reliable default

A **transparent PNG at 300 DPI** is the simplest choice for most DTF work:

- Native transparency tells the RIP exactly where to print and where to generate white.
- Universally supported, easy to produce.
- Best for single designs and most gang sheet builders.

For the vast majority of shops, PNG is the right answer.

## TIFF — maximum quality

**TIFF** is a high-quality raster format that also supports transparency:

- Excellent for detailed or photographic artwork.
- Larger files, but no quality loss.
- A good choice when your RIP prefers it.

## PDF — for layouts and cut lines

**PDF** shines when your file is a **layout** rather than a single image:

- Carries multiple elements, vector cut lines and exact dimensions reliably.
- Ideal for [gang sheets](/blog/how-to-make-a-gang-sheet) and [sticker cut lines](/guides/sticker-cut-lines).
- Make sure rasters inside it are still 300 DPI.

## What to avoid

- **JPEG** — no transparency and lossy compression artefacts. Don't use it for DTF.
- **Low-res anything** — soft, pixelated prints.
- **Flattened backgrounds** — kills the transparency you need.

## The bottom line

Default to a **transparent 300 DPI PNG** for single designs, **TIFF** when you want maximum quality, and **PDF** for layouts with cut lines — and always match your RIP's preference.
