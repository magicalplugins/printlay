DTF and UV DTF sound similar and share a workflow mindset, but they solve different problems. Here's the quick, practical breakdown.

## The core difference

- **DTF (Direct to Film)** → presses onto **fabric** with **heat**. For apparel: t-shirts, hoodies, bags.
- **UV DTF** → sticks to **hard surfaces** with **no heat**. For mugs, bottles, glass, phone cases, signage.

## Side by side

| | DTF | UV DTF |
|---|---|---|
| Surface | Fabric | Hard, smooth surfaces |
| Application | Heat press | Peel & burnish (no heat) |
| Film | PET transfer film | AB film (two-layer) |
| Powder | Adhesive powder | No powder |
| Feel | Soft, flexible | Rigid decal/sticker |
| Best for | Apparel | Hard-goods customisation |

## How the processes differ

**DTF** prints onto PET film, applies adhesive powder, cures it, then heat-presses onto the garment and peels. Full detail in the [DTF printing guide](/guides/dtf-printing).

**UV DTF** prints and UV-cures onto the **A film**, laminates, then a **B film** lifts the design off and applies it to the product — no heat, no water. More in the [UV DTF gang sheets guide](/guides/uv-dtf-gang-sheets).

## What they share

Both rely on **gang sheets** to be profitable: you pack many designs onto one sheet to cut cost per print. The packing rules are the same even though the materials differ — see [what is a gang sheet](/guides/what-is-a-gang-sheet).

## Which should you offer?

Most growing shops end up offering **both** — DTF for apparel, UV DTF for hard goods — because they reuse the same artwork and the same gang-sheet workflow. If you're starting out, lead with whichever matches your customer base, then add the other once volume justifies the second setup.
