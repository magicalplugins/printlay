Pricing is where DTF shops quietly win or lose money. Charge from a gut feeling and you'll either scare customers off or work for free. Here's a simple, defensible way to price transfers and gang sheets.

## 1. Find your true cost per print

Start with what a print actually costs you:

- **Film** — your price per linear metre, at your roll width.
- **Ink** — including the white underbase (your priciest channel).
- **Powder, time and overhead** — a per-sheet allowance.

Then divide by how many designs fit on the sheet. The [gang sheet calculator](/tools/gang-sheet-calculator) does this directly — set your price per metre and design size, and it returns the **cost per print** and **cost per sheet**. The tighter you pack, the lower that number — see [reduce DTF film waste](/blog/reduce-dtf-film-waste).

## 2. Add labour

Design handling, printing, powdering, curing, weeding (for stickers) and packing all take time. Add a realistic per-order labour figure — small one-off orders carry more labour per print than big repeat runs, which is why minimums and quantity breaks exist.

## 3. Choose a margin

On top of cost + labour, apply your margin. A common retail target is a **2–4× markup** on cost (≈60–75% gross margin), trimmed for trade and bulk customers. Decide your floor and don't go below it to "win" a job that then loses money.

## 4. Build a price structure

Translate that into something customers can buy:

- **By the metre** — fixed width, priced per linear metre, with breaks (e.g. cheaper per metre above 5 m).
- **Fixed sheet sizes** — small / medium / large with set prices.
- **Quantity breaks** — reward bigger orders, which are more efficient for you anyway.

## 5. Review as costs change

Film and ink prices move. Revisit your cost per print every few months — or whenever a supplier price changes — and adjust. A 10% film increase you don't pass on comes straight out of your margin.

## The shortcut

Know your numbers before you quote: run your film price per metre and typical design size through the [gang sheet calculator](/tools/gang-sheet-calculator), then apply your margin. For the production side, see [how to make a gang sheet](/blog/how-to-make-a-gang-sheet).
