Custom stickers are one of the easiest print products to add and one of the most profitable per square metre — if you get cut lines, sizing and pricing right. Here's how to start.

## 1. Choose your method

- **Printed vinyl + cutter (print-and-cut)** — the classic route: print, laminate, then [kiss-cut or die-cut](/blog/kiss-cut-vs-die-cut).
- **[UV DTF](/guides/uv-dtf-gang-sheets) stickers** — durable transfer stickers that apply to hard surfaces without a laminate step.

Both reward the same layout discipline: pack many stickers onto one sheet.

## 2. Get cut lines right

This is where most beginners trip up. Every sticker needs a clean **[cut line](/guides/sticker-cut-lines)** (cut contour) so the cutter knows the shape, plus a small bleed so no white edges show. Decide between:

- **[Kiss-cut](/glossary/kiss-cut)** — cuts the sticker but not the backing (peel-off, sheet-style).
- **[Die-cut](/glossary/die-cut)** — cuts all the way through to the sticker's shape.

See [how to add a cut line for stickers](/blog/how-to-add-a-cut-line-for-stickers) for the step-by-step.

## 3. Pick standard sizes

Offer a few set sizes (e.g. **50 mm, 75 mm, 100 mm**) plus a custom option. Standard sizes:

- Let you **gang many stickers** onto one sheet efficiently.
- Keep your pricing and ordering simple for customers.

## 4. Price from real cost

Don't guess. Work out your **cost per sticker**:

1. Media + ink cost per sheet (use your price per metre).
2. Divide by how many stickers fit the sheet.
3. Add labour — cutting, weeding, packing.
4. Add your margin.

The [gang sheet calculator](/tools/gang-sheet-calculator) does the per-sheet maths for you, and the same [pricing approach as DTF transfers](/blog/how-to-price-dtf-transfers) applies.

## 5. Make ordering easy

Standard sizes, clear artwork requirements, and a fast turnaround win repeat business. The more you can **standardise and automate the layout**, the more orders you can handle without adding hours.

## The takeaway

Stickers are high-margin when your cut lines are clean, your sizes are standardised, and your sheets are packed. Nail those three and a printer plus a cutter becomes a tidy little profit centre.
