It's the question almost every garment shop asks: **HTV or DTF?** Both decorate fabric with a heat press, but they work completely differently — and the right choice depends entirely on the design. Here's a practical comparison.

## The core difference

- **[HTV](/guides/heat-transfer-vinyl)** — you **cut coloured vinyl** to shape, weed it, and press it. Colour comes from the vinyl.
- **[DTF](/guides/dtf-printing)** — you **print** the design (in full colour, with a white underbase) onto film, powder it, cure it, and press it.

That single difference drives everything else.

## Cost

| | HTV | DTF |
|---|---|---|
| **Simple 1-colour design** | Cheaper | More setup overhead |
| **Full-colour / multi-colour** | Expensive & slow (layering) | Cheaper & faster |
| **Consumables** | Vinyl only | Film, ink (incl. white), powder |

For a single-colour name on a shirt, HTV usually wins. For a photographic chest print, DTF wins by a mile.

## Colour and detail

- **HTV** — bold, opaque solid colours; limited to what you can cut and layer. No gradients or photos.
- **DTF** — unlimited colours, gradients, fine detail and photographic images in one pass.

## Speed and labour

- **HTV** — every colour is a separate cut + weed + press. Fine for one or two colours; painful beyond that.
- **DTF** — one print, one press, regardless of how many colours. Scales much better for complex art, especially when you [gang up many designs](/guides/what-is-a-gang-sheet) on a sheet.

## Specialty finishes

HTV has the edge here — **glitter, flock, holographic, reflective and puff** vinyls create textures and effects DTF can't reproduce.

## The verdict

Don't pick one forever — **pick per job**:

- **Choose HTV** for solid-colour names, numbers, simple logos, and specialty-finish work.
- **Choose DTF** for full-colour, multi-colour, gradient or photographic designs, and anything you want to produce in volume.

Most successful shops run both. If your work is mostly full-colour, lean DTF and get your [gang sheet workflow](/blog/how-to-make-a-gang-sheet) tight — that's where the margin is. If you're also comparing DTF to hard-surface transfers, see [DTF vs UV DTF](/blog/dtf-vs-uv-dtf).
