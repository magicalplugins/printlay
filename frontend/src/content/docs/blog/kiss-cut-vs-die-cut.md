Kiss-cut and die-cut are the two ways to cut a printed sticker. They use the same printer and cutter — the difference is **how deep the blade goes**.

## The difference in one line

- **Kiss-cut** — cuts the **vinyl only**, leaving the paper backing intact.
- **Die-cut** — cuts through **vinyl and backing**, producing an individual sticker.

## Kiss-cut

The blade "kisses" through the vinyl but not the backing, so stickers stay put on the sheet and peel off one at a time.

**Best for:** sticker sheets, multi-packs, label sheets, anything where stickers stay together until use.

**Pros:** easy to handle, ship flat, no fiddly singles. **Cons:** you keep the backing rectangle, so the final shape isn't standalone.

## Die-cut

The blade cuts all the way through, giving you a standalone sticker cut to the design's shape (usually with a small border).

**Best for:** individual custom stickers, premium singles, irregular shapes.

**Pros:** clean standalone product. **Cons:** more weeding/handling for singles.

## How it affects your cut line

Both use a **cut contour** on the CutContour spot colour — the cutter just uses a different blade depth/force. So your file prep is essentially the same: trace the contour, add bleed, assign the spot. See the [sticker cut lines guide](/guides/sticker-cut-lines) and [how to add a cut line for stickers](/blog/how-to-add-a-cut-line-for-stickers).

## On a gang sheet

You can gang up either type — kiss-cut sheets are especially efficient because the whole sheet is the product. A [sticker cut line generator](/features/sticker-cut-line-generator) builds the contours and a gang sheet builder packs them tightly so the cutter does it all in one pass.
