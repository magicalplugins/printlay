In DTF, **empty film is money in the bin**. Every square inch you don't use is film, white ink and printer time you paid for. Here's how to waste less of it.

## 1. Gang up everything

The biggest lever by far: pack many designs (or many copies) onto **one sheet** instead of running them separately. This spreads fixed cost across more prints and leaves less empty film. If gang sheets are new, start with [what is a gang sheet](/guides/what-is-a-gang-sheet).

## 2. Nest tightly, not in a grid

A rigid grid leaves triangular gaps around odd shapes. **True nesting** — mixing sizes and rotating designs to fill those gaps — fits far more on each sheet. Going from a loose grid to tight nesting can easily **double** designs per sheet.

## 3. Use the minimum safe gap

Bigger gaps mean fewer designs per sheet. Use the **smallest gap** your cutter allows for separating transfers cleanly — typically 3–5 mm for DTF. Consistency matters: uneven gaps waste space and complicate cutting.

## 4. Standardise design sizes

Random sizes tile badly. Rounding artwork to a few **standard sizes** makes nesting predictable and tight. See the [gang sheet size guide](/blog/gang-sheet-size-guide).

## 5. Fill the length

If you sell DTF by length, fill the **full length** you're charging for before sending it. Half-empty sheets are pure waste.

## The maths

Say a sheet costs £8 and you fit 20 designs — that's 40p each. Nest better and fit 40 — now it's **20p each**. On 500 sheets a year that's a meaningful chunk of margin recovered from thin air.
