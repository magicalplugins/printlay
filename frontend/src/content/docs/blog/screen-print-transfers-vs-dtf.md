If you're choosing how to decorate apparel, **screen print transfers** (plastisol transfers) and **[DTF](/guides/dtf-printing)** are two of the most common routes. Both press onto the garment, but the economics and look differ. Here's how to choose.

## What each one is

- **Screen print transfer** — a design **screen-printed with [plastisol](/glossary/plastisol) ink** onto release paper, then heat-pressed onto garments later. Classic screen-print feel; colours separated like traditional screen printing.
- **DTF** — a **digitally printed** transfer on film, full colour with a white underbase, powdered and cured.

## Cost and run length

This is the deciding factor:

- **Screen print transfers** carry **screen setup costs** per colour, so they're expensive for small runs but very cheap per print at high volume with few colours.
- **DTF** has **no setup** and colour count doesn't change the price, so it wins for short runs and full-colour art — but per-print cost doesn't drop as steeply at huge volumes.

**Rule of thumb:** big runs, few colours → screen print transfers. Short runs or full colour → DTF.

## Colour and detail

- **Screen print transfers** — best for bold, limited-colour designs; each colour adds a screen and cost.
- **DTF** — unlimited colours, gradients and photographic detail in one pass.

## Feel and durability

- **Screen print transfers** — the classic, slightly heavier plastisol hand; extremely durable.
- **DTF** — thin, flexible, stretchy; very durable when [applied and cured correctly](/blog/dtf-transfer-troubleshooting).

## The verdict

- **Choose screen print transfers** for large runs of simple, few-colour designs where the classic feel matters.
- **Choose DTF** for short runs, on-demand orders, and full-colour or photographic artwork — and lean on [gang sheets](/guides/what-is-a-gang-sheet) to keep your cost per transfer down.
