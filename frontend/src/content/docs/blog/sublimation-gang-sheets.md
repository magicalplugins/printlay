Sublimation is another print method that benefits hugely from **ganging up** — packing many designs onto one sheet of sublimation paper to save paper, ink and press cycles. If you already gang up DTF, the layout thinking is identical.

## How sublimation differs from DTF

- **No white ink** — sublimation has no underbase, so it only works on **light polyester or poly-coated** items (mugs, light garments, coasters), dyeing the surface rather than sitting on it.
- **Sublimation paper, not film** — you print onto special paper, then heat-press so the ink turns to gas and bonds into the substrate.
- **Substrate-limited** — it won't work on cotton or dark items, unlike [DTF](/guides/dtf-printing).

For the broader comparison of transfer methods, see [DTF vs UV DTF](/blog/dtf-vs-uv-dtf).

## Why gang up sublimation prints

The same logic as any [gang sheet](/guides/what-is-a-gang-sheet): you pay for the paper, ink and press time per sheet, so filling the sheet lowers the cost of each design and cuts waste.

## Laying out a sublimation gang sheet

- **Match the sheet to your printer width** and the press platen you'll use.
- **Keep consistent gaps** so designs cut apart cleanly.
- **Mirror where needed** — many sublimation workflows print mirrored; check your process.
- **Nest tightly** — mix sizes to fill gaps, just like DTF.

The mechanics of building the sheet are the same as in [how to make a gang sheet](/blog/how-to-make-a-gang-sheet), and you can check fit and paper cost with the [gang sheet calculator](/tools/gang-sheet-calculator).

## The bottom line

Sublimation, DTF and UV DTF all reward the same discipline: pack the sheet, keep gaps tight, and standardise sizes. One layout habit pays off across every method your shop runs.
