UV DTF only becomes profitable when your **layout** is tight. Because a UV printer lays ink across the sheet whether you fill it or not, every empty patch is wasted ink and film. Here's how to lay out UV print jobs that pack the most usable transfers onto each sheet.

## Why layout matters more for UV

UV ink and film are not cheap, and UV DTF often decorates **small items** (bottle logos, can wraps, phone cases). Lots of small designs means lots of opportunities to waste space between them. Good [UV DTF](/guides/uv-dtf-gang-sheets) layout is the difference between a profitable run and a marginal one.

## The layout principles

- **Fit the sheet to your printer width** — design to the usable print area, not an arbitrary canvas.
- **Consistent gaps** — leave just enough space to separate and cut transfers cleanly. A few millimetres is usually plenty.
- **Nest mixed sizes** — slot small designs into the gaps around larger ones so very little film goes unused. This is [nesting](/glossary/nesting), and it's where most of the savings come from.
- **Mind the cut paths** — if you're cutting or kiss-cutting, keep contours from crowding each other.
- **Orient for efficiency** — rotating a design can sometimes pack more per row.

## Quantify it

Before you print, run your design size, sheet size and film cost through the [gang sheet calculator](/tools/gang-sheet-calculator). UV print layout is the same discipline as any gang sheet, sharpened: small items, pricey ink, so **pack tight, gap consistently, nest mixed sizes**, and let software do the arranging.
