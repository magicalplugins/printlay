Most shops start by building gang sheets **by hand in Photoshop or Illustrator**. It works — but is it the best use of your time? Here's an honest head-to-head with a dedicated [gang sheet builder](/features/gang-sheet-builder).

## Speed

- **Photoshop / Illustrator** — set the canvas, import each design, nudge spacing, draw cut lines, export. Every sheet, every time. Minutes to half an hour per sheet.
- **Builder** — drop in designs, set quantities, done in seconds.

At any real volume, this is the whole ballgame.

## Nesting and film efficiency

- **By hand** — you nest as well as your patience allows, and usually leave more gaps than you realise.
- **Builder** — packs mixed sizes automatically and tightly, squeezing more transfers onto each sheet. That directly [cuts film waste](/blog/reduce-dtf-film-waste).

## Cut lines

- **By hand** — draw or offset paths manually for every sticker. Slow and easy to get wrong.
- **Builder** — generates [cut lines](/guides/sticker-cut-lines) automatically.

## Errors

- **By hand** — wrong DPI, inconsistent gaps, missed bleed, forgotten white layer. Each one risks a reprint.
- **Builder** — enforces consistent spacing and print-ready output, removing the most common mistakes.

## Cost

- **Photoshop / Illustrator** — you may already pay for it, but the real cost is **your time** per sheet.
- **Builder** — a tool cost that pays back quickly in saved hours and reduced waste.

## Where Illustrator still wins

Dedicated builders arrange sheets; they don't **create artwork**. You still need a design app to draw, edit or fix the designs themselves. The smart workflow is **both**: design in Illustrator, **lay out in a builder**.

## The verdict

If you build sheets more than occasionally, doing it by hand is costing you time and film you can't see. Keep your design app for artwork, and hand the repetitive layout to a builder.
