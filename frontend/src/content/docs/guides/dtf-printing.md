**DTF** — Direct to Film — has become the default way to print full-colour designs onto apparel in short runs. It's flexible, it works on almost any fabric, and it doesn't need screens or weeding. But the economics only work when you **gang up** your designs onto efficient sheets. Here's how the whole process fits together.

## How DTF printing works

DTF is a five-step process:

1. **Print** — the design is printed onto **PET transfer film** using DTF inks (CMYK plus white).
2. **Powder** — while the ink is still wet, **adhesive powder** is applied and the excess shaken off.
3. **Cure** — the powder is melted/cured with heat (an oven or heat press hover) so it bonds to the ink.
4. **Press** — the film is heat-pressed onto the garment.
5. **Peel** — once cooled (or hot, depending on film), the film peels away leaving the design.

The result is a durable, stretchy, full-colour print that works on cotton, polyester and blends alike.

## Why DTF and gang sheets go together

A DTF printer lays ink across the **full width of the film** regardless of how much of it you use. Print one small logo and you've wasted most of the film and a chunk of white ink. That's why virtually every DTF shop prints onto **gang sheets** — packing many designs (or many copies of one design) onto a single run of film.

- Spreads media, ink and printer time across many transfers.
- Cuts white-ink usage per design — your most expensive consumable.
- Reduces film offcuts and re-runs.

If gang sheets are new to you, start with [what is a gang sheet](/guides/what-is-a-gang-sheet).

## Equipment and consumables

A working DTF setup needs:

- **Printer** — a dedicated DTF printer or a converted model running DTF inks (including white).
- **Film** — PET transfer film, hot- or cold-peel.
- **Powder** — hot-melt adhesive powder.
- **Curing** — an oven, conveyor, or careful heat-press curing.
- **Heat press** — for the final application.
- **Layout software** — to nest designs into print-ready gang sheets with the right spacing and resolution.

## Getting print-ready files right

DTF is unforgiving of bad files. Three things to check before every sheet:

- **Resolution** — 300 DPI at final print size. Use our [bleed & DPI calculator](/tools/bleed-dpi-calculator) if you're unsure.
- **White layer** — a correct white underbase is what makes colours pop on dark fabric.
- **Spacing** — consistent gaps so transfers cut apart cleanly.

## DTF vs UV DTF

DTF is for **fabric**; **UV DTF** is for **hard surfaces** (mugs, bottles, cases) and needs no heat. Many shops run both. We break down the differences in [DTF vs UV DTF](/blog/dtf-vs-uv-dtf), and cover hard-goods layout in the [UV DTF gang sheets guide](/guides/uv-dtf-gang-sheets).

## Making DTF profitable

The margin in DTF lives in two places: **white-ink discipline** and **sheet packing**. You can't do much about the first beyond good colour management — but the second is entirely in your control. Pack your sheets tight, standardise your sizes, and automate the layout so you're not losing an hour a day arranging artwork.
