**Heat transfer vinyl** (HTV, also called iron-on or T-shirt vinyl) is one of the oldest and most reliable ways to decorate apparel. Instead of printing ink, you **cut coloured vinyl to shape**, weed away what you don't want, and press it onto fabric. Here's how it fits alongside printed methods like [DTF](/guides/dtf-printing).

## How HTV works

1. **Design** — create or import a **vector** design (the cutter follows paths, not pixels).
2. **Cut** — a vinyl cutter cuts the design into a sheet of HTV. You cut **mirrored**, because you press it face-down.
3. **Weed** — remove the excess vinyl, leaving just your design on its carrier sheet.
4. **Press** — heat-press onto the garment; the adhesive backing bonds the vinyl to the fabric.
5. **Peel** — peel the carrier (hot or cold depending on the vinyl).

The colour is the vinyl itself, which is why HTV produces bold, opaque, hard-wearing results.

## Where HTV beats printed transfers

- **Solid colours** — names, numbers, simple logos and text look crisp and last for years.
- **Specialty finishes** — glitter, flock, holographic, reflective and puff vinyls do things ink can't.
- **Low cost per simple job** — no white ink, no powder; just vinyl and press time.

## Where it struggles (and DTF wins)

- **Full-colour or photographic** designs — you'd need to cut and layer many colours, which is slow and fiddly.
- **Fine detail and gradients** — hard to weed and can't reproduce smooth tonal shifts.

For those, a printed transfer like DTF is the better tool. See the full breakdown in [HTV vs DTF](/blog/htv-vs-dtf).

## The kit you need

- **Vinyl cutter / plotter** with the right blade and force settings.
- **HTV** in rolls or sheets, in the colours and finishes you offer.
- **Weeding tools** and a light pad help a lot.
- **Heat press** with reliable, even temperature and pressure.

## Cut lines and layout

HTV lives and dies by clean **cut paths**. Your artwork needs to be vector with well-defined contours — the same discipline that matters for [sticker cut lines](/guides/sticker-cut-lines). Lay multiple designs across a sheet to use the vinyl efficiently, much like ganging up printed transfers.

## HTV or DTF — or both?

Most thriving garment shops run **both**: HTV for solid-colour names/numbers and specialty finishes, DTF for full-colour artwork. Pick the method per job rather than forcing everything through one. If you're weighing them up, read [HTV vs DTF](/blog/htv-vs-dtf) next.
