**UV printing** uses inks that **cure instantly under ultraviolet light** rather than drying by evaporation or heat. The moment the ink hits the UV lamp it hardens — which is what lets it stick to materials that other inks can't. Here's how it works and where it fits.

## How UV printing works

A UV printer lays down ink that's immediately set by **UV LED lamps** as it prints. Because curing is instant and the ink sits on the surface, UV bonds to a vast range of **rigid and non-porous materials**:

- Plastic, acrylic and PVC
- Metal and glass
- Wood, card and leather

That versatility makes UV the go-to for signage, promotional products, packaging and hard-goods decoration.

## UV printing vs UV DTF

Direct UV printing needs the item to sit **flat on a bed**, which rules out curved or awkward shapes. That's where **[UV DTF](/guides/uv-dtf-gang-sheets)** comes in:

- Print the design onto a **UV DTF transfer film**.
- Apply it to curved or 3D items — bottles, mugs, cases, tumblers — with pressure, **no heat required**.

UV DTF brings UV's material versatility to objects you could never run through a flatbed. For the apparel-vs-hard-goods picture, see [DTF vs UV DTF](/blog/dtf-vs-uv-dtf).

## Laying out UV jobs efficiently

Like any print process, UV rewards **tight layout**. Whether you're printing rigid panels or UV DTF film, packing multiple designs onto one sheet spreads ink and media cost across more pieces. See [UV print layout](/blog/uv-print-layout) for how to nest UV DTF designs, and check fit and cost with the [gang sheet calculator](/tools/gang-sheet-calculator).

## The takeaway

UV printing is about **versatility** — decorating materials that heat and solvent inks can't touch. Pair direct UV for flat rigid work with UV DTF for everything curved, and you can decorate almost any surface a customer brings you.
