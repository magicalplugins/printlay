A **gang sheet** is one large printable sheet filled with many designs at once. Instead of printing each logo, name or sticker on its own, you "gang up" dozens of them onto a single sheet, print it in one pass, then cut the designs apart. It's the backbone of efficient DTF, UV DTF and sticker production — and the single biggest lever on your cost per print.

## Why print shops gang up sheets

The cost of running a print isn't really about the design — it's about the **sheet**. You pay for media (film or vinyl), ink, printer time and labour roughly per sheet, whether that sheet carries one design or fifty.

So the maths is simple: the more usable area you fill, the lower the cost of each individual design.

- **Lower cost per print.** A 3-inch logo printed alone wastes most of the sheet. Forty of them on one sheet share the same fixed cost.
- **Less wasted film.** Tight packing means fewer offcuts heading for the bin.
- **Fewer print runs.** One sheet through the printer instead of forty separate jobs.
- **Faster turnaround.** Print once, cut once, ship.

## What goes on a gang sheet

Gang sheets are most associated with:

- **DTF transfers** — heat-press transfers printed onto PET film, then powdered and cured.
- **UV DTF transfers** — stickers/decals on AB film applied to hard surfaces with no heat.
- **Die-cut and kiss-cut stickers** — many shapes nested onto one vinyl sheet.

In every case the goal is the same: fit as many designs as possible into the printable area while leaving enough gap to cut them apart cleanly.

## Anatomy of a good gang sheet

A well-built gang sheet balances density with practicality:

1. **Sheet size** — set to your printer or roll width (22 inches / ~56 cm is the most common DTF width).
2. **Gaps** — a consistent margin between designs so the cutter or scissors have room (typically 3–5 mm).
3. **Orientation** — designs rotated where it helps them tile more tightly.
4. **Bleed and cut lines** — for stickers, artwork extends past the cut and a contour line marks where to cut.
5. **Resolution** — every design placed at 300 DPI at final size so nothing prints soft.

## How to build a gang sheet

There are two ways to do it:

**The hard way — by hand in Illustrator or Photoshop.** You create a canvas at the sheet size, drag each design in, nudge spacing, rotate to fit, and check resolution manually. It works, but it's slow, error-prone and the reason so many shop owners are still arranging artwork at 2am.

## Common gang sheet sizes

Sizes vary by printer and material, but these are the ones you'll meet most often:

- **22" wide** (roll-fed DTF) by any length — the industry standard width.
- **A3 / 11.7" × 16.5"** — common for smaller batches and UV DTF.
- **12" × 12"** — a popular fixed UV DTF tile.

## Gang sheets and cost: a worked example

Say a sheet costs you £8 in media and ink, and you can fit 40 three-inch designs on it. That's **20p per print** before labour. Drop to 10 designs per sheet because of sloppy spacing, and the same designs now cost **80p each** — four times as much. Packing efficiency is real money.

## The bottom line

Gang sheets turn a stack of small jobs into one efficient print run. Get the packing tight, the gaps consistent and the cut lines clean, and you'll cut both cost and turnaround dramatically. The only question is whether you arrange them by hand — or let software do it in seconds.
